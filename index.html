ICE GLOBAL:

When I first joined ICE, I thought my job would be pretty straightforward — test features, report bugs, and keep things moving. But very quickly, I realized this place was different. QA wasn’t a quiet corner where we just tick boxes. At ICE, quality was everyone’s job, and I had the opportunity to become the voice of that quality.

I didn’t just test tickets. I lived inside them. I read every word of the user stories, challenged unclear requirements, double-checked designs, and chased down edge cases no one else had even thought about. I wanted to make sure that when something left our hands, it actually made sense for the people using it, whether they were resellers, customers, or internal teams.

One of my biggest lessons came when the QA lead went on leave. I could have just quietly done my own work, but instead, I stepped up and took the wheel. I ran daily standups, monitored every channel, followed up with developers, product managers, and designers, and made sure nothing slipped through the cracks. It wasn’t easy — but that week taught me that leadership isn’t a title. It’s about showing up when you’re needed the most.

There was also this one sprint I’ll never forget. Our delivery rate had been hovering around 56% — not terrible, but not great either. That sprint, we made it to 86%. And I knew it wasn’t magic. It happened because we worked smarter, communicated better, and I made sure QA wasn’t just blocking tickets but actively helping developers move faster by being clear, proactive, and hands-on.

But my role at ICE wasn’t just about speed. It was about caring. I didn’t just want to sign off on a ticket because it "technically worked." I cared about how it felt for the user. Did the flow make sense? Was the error message clear? Could a reseller figure this out on their own at midnight when no one was around to help? That mindset shaped every conversation I had — whether I was pushing back on a design, adding clarifications to a ticket, or jumping into a Slack thread to explain why a small detail actually mattered.

Another thing I’m proud of is the documentation footprint I left behind. Every process, every tricky flow, every lesson learned — I documented it. Not because someone asked me to, but because I wanted the next person joining QA to have a smoother ride than I did. To me, documentation is how you say: "I care about the future of this team."

And through all of this, I realized that being a great QA isn’t about being the person who says "no" the loudest. It’s about being the person who asks the right questions, who connects the dots between teams, who sees risks before they happen, and who stands up for quality, even when timelines get tight and pressure runs high.

Some Wins I’m Proud Of

  • Stepped in to lead QA efforts when the QA lead was away, managing all follow-ups, handovers, and processes
  • Owned and improved QA for features like the Mentorship Program, Reseller Journey, Fulfillment Process, and Wallet Management
  • Helped the team go from 56% delivery to 86% in one sprint by improving the way QA worked with Product and Engineering
  • Found and escalated critical production issues, some of which were fixed within hours thanks to fast reactions and thorough investigations
  • Created clear, practical documentation to make onboarding easier and processes smoother for future teammates
  • Worked across Product, Design, and Engineering to close gaps and prevent issues before they reached production
  • Balanced speed and quality by focusing on what really mattered — the user’s experience

At the end of the day, my time at ICE wasn’t just about being a QA. It was about becoming someone the team could depend on, someone who wasn’t afraid to ask uncomfortable questions, and someone who never forgot that every ticket was more than just a task — it was a small part of someone’s experience with our product.

That’s the kind of QA I am. And that’s the kind of QA I’ll continue to be.



Machine Learning journey

I started this all because of a simple CAPTCHA. To address this more accurately, my interest in embarking on a journey in Computer Science stems from the early years of my career as a QA engineer. While writing a routine automation test for a login page on a website, I noticed that the test run could not proceed any further, simply because of a CAPTCHA which was at the bottom of the page. This made me curious to research about CAPTCHA and its underlying technology. This exploration led me to understand the principles of image processing, pattern recognition, and optical character recognition (OCR) that underpin CAPTCHA systems. Moving on, I thought to myself if a robot/machine could learn the details of a picture and make a decision based on that, then what are the other applications of this magnificent technology and its applications. Fascinated by this technology, I delved into the realm of Augmented Reality (AR) and Virtual Reality (VR) and continued the research. Regarding that I hold 2 years of experience in the game industry as a Game designer and a QA Engineer, I knew for a fact that if I could dive deeper into Computer Vision as an academic study, I could understand and utilize this technology in a more advanced sense. Ultimately, I would be able to apply what I have gained, into the development of more immersive and realistic games utilizing computer vision, specifically AR and VR.
